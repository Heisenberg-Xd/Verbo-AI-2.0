Inflation's Uneven Toll: Why the CPI Undercounts the Pain for Average Families

When government economists say inflation has fallen to 3 percent, they are reporting a precise and carefully constructed number. They are also, critics argue, describing an economic reality that few average households recognize from their daily experience at grocery stores, gas stations, and utility bill payment screens.

The Consumer Price Index measures price changes in a fixed basket of goods and services purchased by a typical urban consumer. The basket is comprehensive — including housing, food, transportation, medical care, recreation, and education — and the methodology is rigorous, regularly updated through Consumer Expenditure Surveys to reflect evolving spending patterns. But the headline number obscures enormous variation by product category and by income level.

Grocery prices, which had risen over 20 percent cumulatively between early 2020 and late 2023, remained elevated even as grocery inflation measured year-over-year began moderating. The annual rate of change in food at home peaked above 13 percent in mid-2022. By late 2023, that rate had fallen toward one to two percent — but this meant that prices were still rising, just more slowly. The compounding of 20 percent cumulative food inflation into household budgets was permanent absent actual price declines, which are rare and typically signal demand destruction rather than benign supply recovery.

Housing costs represent approximately one-third of the CPI basket and pose a particular measurement challenge. The Bureau of Labor Statistics measures housing inflation through a concept called Owners' Equivalent Rent — an estimate of what homeowners would pay to rent their own homes. Critics argue this dramatically understates the shelter inflation experienced by actual renters and prospective homebuyers during periods of rapid rent increases. Real-time rent data from Zillow and ApartmentList showed apartment rents rising 25 to 30 percent in some markets between 2020 and 2022, while CPI shelter inflation lagged significantly due to the survey methodology smoothing contract renewals over time.

The inflationary experience also differs sharply by income level. Lower-income households spend a larger share of their budget on food, energy, and rent — all of which experienced above-average inflation. Higher-income households have larger shares of budget directed toward discretionary services and financial assets, categories where inflation has been more moderate and which benefit from asset price appreciation during the same period. Studies from the National Bureau of Economic Research found that the effective inflation rate faced by the lowest income quintile exceeded that faced by the highest income quintile by two to three percentage points during peak inflation — a distributional consequence that the headline CPI number does not reveal.
