The Dollar's Global Dominance: Challenged but Undefeated

The US dollar's status as the world's primary reserve currency has been a defining feature of global finance since the Bretton Woods agreements that followed World War II. Roughly 58 percent of global foreign exchange reserves are held in dollars. Around 54 percent of global trade invoicing uses dollars even when neither trading party is American. Approximately 88 percent of global foreign exchange market transactions involve dollars. The SWIFT messaging system, which underpins global interbank payments, denominated the vast majority of cross-border transactions in dollars.

This dominance confers extraordinary privileges on the United States — the ability to run persistent current account deficits that would be impossible for other nations, to borrow in its own currency, and to exercise economic leverage through sanctions. The freezing of approximately $300 billion in Russian central bank reserves following the invasion of Ukraine in 2022 was a dramatic demonstration of dollar system power — and of its potential consequences for countries that view themselves as potential future sanction targets.

The reaction set off a genuine debate about dollar hegemony. China accelerated efforts to expand renminbi usage in bilateral trade agreements, establishing currency swap lines with dozens of central banks and negotiating commodity contracts — particularly in oil — denominated in yuan. The BRICS nations discussed the creation of a common currency, though the fundamental incompatibilities between their economic systems made any serious near-term implementation implausible. Saudi Arabia, which had priced oil exclusively in dollars under arrangements dating to the 1970s, was reported to be in discussions about yuan-denominated oil sales with China.

Objective analysis finds the dollar's position challenged at the margins but structurally intact. Alternatives face fundamental obstacles. The euro is plagued by political fragmentation and the absence of a common bond market deep enough to accommodate reserve demand. The renminbi is not freely convertible and operates within a capital account that restricts international flows — a fundamental barrier to reserve currency status. Gold is finite and illiquid. Cryptocurrencies lack the institutional infrastructure and stability properties required for large-scale reserve management.

The dollar's dominance is ultimately a reflection of US economic weight, legal system depth, financial market liquidity, and geopolitical centrality — advantages that have eroded somewhat over decades but remain without serious peer. The more consequential risk to dollar dominance is probably domestic fiscal sustainability — the long-run trajectory of US debt-to-GDP — rather than any external rival's currency ambitions.

Central banks including those of China, India, Russia, and Brazil have modestly diversified their reserve portfolios toward gold and other currencies in recent years. This diversification is real but has yet to move the needle materially on the fundamental dollar architecture of global finance. The reign of King Dollar, at least for now, continues by default.
