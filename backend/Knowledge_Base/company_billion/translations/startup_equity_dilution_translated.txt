Startup Equity 101: Dilution, Cap Tables, and What Founders Often Learn Too Late

Founding a company and growing it through multiple rounds of venture capital financing is a process in which ownership percentages almost always go down, company value usually goes up, and the net effect — if the company succeeds — is that founders end up owning less of a much more valuable entity. Understanding the mathematics of this dilution, and the structural mechanics built into term sheets that drive it, is among the most practically important competencies a founder can develop.

The capitalization table, universally shortened to cap table, is the living record of who owns what percentage of a company. At founding, it might show two co-founders owning 50 percent each. After a seed round in which investors receive 20 percent, both founders hold 40 percent, and the new investors hold 20. After a Series A where another 20 percent goes to investors, everyone on the pre-deal cap table is diluted proportionally — the founders now hold approximately 32 percent each, and so on through subsequent rounds.

Options reserved for employees — the option pool — add another dimension of dilution that founders consistently underestimate. Venture investors typically require a 10 to 20 percent fully diluted option pool to be created before closing a round, meaning that the dilution from creating the pool falls entirely on the pre-money cap table. A term sheet offering a $10 million pre-money valuation with a mandatory 15 percent option pool expansion means that the effective pre-money value attributed to the founders is reduced by the amount of the option pool, a practice that advocates argue is reasonable for attracting talent but critics describe as a hidden form of investor-favorable pricing mechanics.

Protective provisions — a standard feature of preferred stock issued to venture investors — give investors the right to block certain company actions without their consent. These typically include selling the company, raising additional capital, issuing new shares, paying dividends, and changing the company's certificate of incorporation. These provisions exist to protect investors from actions that could harm their investment, but they also give investors meaningful control over strategic decisions even when their ownership percentage is minority.

Liquidation preferences determine who gets paid first when a company exits. Participating preferred stock — which allows investors to first receive their invested capital back and then participate pro-rata in remaining proceeds — can dramatically reduce founder and common stockholder returns in moderate-outcome exits. In a company that raises $20 million on a participating preferred structure and sells for $40 million, investors take their $20 million back first then participate in the remaining $20 million proportionally — an outcome structurally superior to what a straightforward common shareholder receives.

The consistent advice from founders who have navigated multiple fundraising cycles is to engage an experienced startup attorney before signing any term sheet, to understand every protective provision and preference in detail, and to model dilution scenarios across multiple exit outcomes before agreeing to terms. The decisions made in early rounds compound in ways that are not obvious when signing but become painfully clear in the exit waterfall.
