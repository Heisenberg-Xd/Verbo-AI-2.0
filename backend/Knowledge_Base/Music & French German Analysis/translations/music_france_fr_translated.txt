The French music industry between cultural heritage and digital revolution

France occupies a unique position in the global musical landscape, at the intersection of centuries-old artistic traditions and a creative industry fully engaged in digital transformation. French song, carried by icons like Édith Piaf, Jacques Brel and Serge Gainsbourg, constitutes an intangible heritage recognized internationally, while new French-speaking artists continually redefine the contours of this musical identity. The emergence of contemporary artists such as Stromae, Angèle, and Aya Nakamura has demonstrated that music that is resolutely French in its language and influences can conquer international markets. Aya Nakamura has thus become the most streamed French-speaking artist worldwide, with billions of streams on digital platforms. This success demonstrates a global appetite for hybrid sounds mixing Afrobeats, pop and traditional songs. French cultural policy plays a structuring role in the national music industry. Radio broadcast quotas, which impose a minimum of forty percent of French-speaking songs on the air, are regularly the subject of debate between defenders of cultural diversity and supporters of liberalization of programs. The National Music Center distributes tens of millions of euros each year in subsidies and aid for creation, live performance and the export of French talent. Music festivals have become true cultural institutions in France. The Vieilles Charrues in Brittany, Rock en Seine in Paris, and the Francofolies in La Rochelle attract hundreds of thousands of spectators every summer. These events play a considerable economic role for their territories, while serving as a showcase for the contemporary French music scene. French musical training, with its national and regional conservatories, continues to train world-class musicians. Traditional classical education coexists with new training in electronic production, image composition and music management, meeting the demands of an industry in constant change.
