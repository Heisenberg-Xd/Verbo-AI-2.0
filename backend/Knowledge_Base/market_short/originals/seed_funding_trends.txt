Seed Funding in 2024: How the Early-Stage Market Held Up When Late-Stage Collapsed

One of the more counterintuitive developments in the venture capital contraction of 2022 and 2023 was that seed-stage funding held up far better than late-stage investing. While Series B and beyond saw dramatic volume declines and valuation resets, the seed market remained active — a resilience explained by the mathematics of early-stage investing and the behavior of the investor class most active at that stage.

Seed checks are small by venture standards, typically ranging from $500,000 to $3 million from individual angels and seed funds, or up to $5 million in institutional seed rounds. The diligence burden is lower, the time to portfolio company outcome is longer, and the entry valuation multiples are modest enough that even in a compressed public market environment, a seed investor can still construct a plausible path to a return. Buying ten percent of a pre-revenue company at a $10 million valuation requires only a $100 million outcome — a threshold that the market can absorb even when unicorn fantasies are out of fashion.

Micro-VC funds — vehicles with less than $100 million under management, each deploying small checks across large portfolios — proliferated dramatically over the prior decade. By 2023, there were over 800 active micro-VCs in the United States alone. Their existence created a competitive seed market in which founders with credible backgrounds and compelling ideas could still raise capital even as the Series A and beyond pipelines tightened dramatically.

The terms of seed deals shifted. Pre-money valuations, which had reached absurd levels in 2021 — with pre-revenue companies regularly pricing at $15 to $20 million — moderated toward $8 to $12 million ranges. Pro-rata rights, which give investors the option to maintain their percentage ownership in subsequent rounds, became more heavily negotiated as late-stage scarcity made future access more uncertain. Information rights and board observer seats were more routinely granted as investors sought greater visibility into portfolio company performance.

The strategic calculus for founders shifted alongside the funding environment. The "raise as much as possible at the highest valuation possible" mentality of 2021 gave way to a more austere philosophy emphasizing capital efficiency, sustainable unit economics, and proving business model assumptions before raising larger rounds. Founders who had learned from watching 2021 vintage peers run out of runway at high burn rates approached their 2023 and 2024 funding rounds with more conservative plans and longer runways in mind.

For seed investors, the market offered genuine opportunity. The combination of lower entry valuations, less competition from crossover and growth funds that had retreated up-market, and a generation of founders chastened by the capital scarcity of 2022 to 2023 created what experienced practitioners described as one of the most attractive seed vintage sets in years. Whether that conviction proves correct will be knowable only around 2028 through 2030, when those investments approach their natural liquidity windows.
