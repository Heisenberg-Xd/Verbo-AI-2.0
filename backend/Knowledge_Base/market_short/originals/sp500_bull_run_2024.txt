The S&P 500 Bull Run: What's Driving Markets to Record Highs in 2024

The S&P 500 crossed 5,000 points for the first time in February 2024, extending a bull market that had confounded the recession forecasters who spent much of 2022 and 2023 warning of imminent economic collapse. The index finished 2023 up 26.3 percent, with 2024 picking up where the prior year left off as earnings beat expectations and inflation data trended in the right direction.

Several forces converged to produce this relentless upward grind. The Federal Reserve's pivot from aggressive rate hikes to prospective cuts, signaled clearly at its December 2023 meeting, removed one of the key headwinds that had battered valuations throughout 2022. Lower expected rates pushed the discount rate applied to future earnings lower, mechanically boosting the present value of equity cash flows, particularly for growth stocks trading on long-duration earnings.

The artificial intelligence investment cycle was perhaps the single most powerful driver. Nvidia's extraordinary earnings growth — with data center revenue surging fourfold year over year — demonstrated that AI capital expenditure was translating into real revenue for the infrastructure layer. Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta each reported accelerating cloud and AI revenue in their quarterly results, providing fundamental justification for what skeptics dismissed as multiple expansion.

The market's concentration in a handful of mega-cap technology companies became a frequent subject of concern. The so-called Magnificent Seven — Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Nvidia, Meta, and Tesla — collectively represented over 30 percent of the S&P 500's market capitalization. When these stocks rally, the index rallies. When they stumble, the index stumbles. Critics argue this concentration creates fragility; bulls counter that these companies earn it through genuinely superior returns on capital.

Breadth remained a legitimate worry through the first quarter of 2024. The equal-weighted version of the S&P 500, which assigns each of the 500 stocks the same weight rather than weighting by market cap, significantly underperformed the headline index. This meant that the majority of stocks were not participating proportionately in the rally — a condition that has historically resolved either by narrowing (the big stocks correcting) or broadening (smaller stocks catching up).

For long-term investors, the message of 2024 was familiar: timing the market is less productive than time in the market. Those who sold in 2022 fear and held cash through 2023 missed one of the strongest years in recent memory.
