Unicorn Valuations Under Pressure: The Painful Markdown Era

The term "unicorn" — a private technology company valued at $1 billion or more — was coined in 2013 when such companies were genuinely rare. By 2021, there were over 900 globally, a proliferation made possible by a decade of cheap capital, investor FOMO, and the willingness of late-stage funds to rationalize almost any valuation that projected a large enough total addressable market.

The reckoning arrived with remarkable speed. As public technology company valuations collapsed between late 2021 and mid-2022 — the Nasdaq fell 33 percent peak to trough — the theoretical framework undergirding venture-stage valuations evaporated. The prevailing convention of valuing late-stage startups at revenue multiples comparable to public software companies became untenable when those public company multiples contracted by 50 to 80 percent.

The awkward problem for venture capital was accounting treatment. Unlike public market investors who face daily mark-to-market losses, venture funds are only required to mark down private portfolio companies when there is a "significant observable transaction" suggesting a lower value. The result was a period of widespread "extend and pretend" in which fund managers reported portfolios at 2021 peak valuations while quietly acknowledging in LP meetings that the true mark-to-market values were substantially lower.

Observable markdowns accumulated through 2022 and 2023 as companies raised new funding at flat or down rounds. Klarna, the Swedish buy-now-pay-later company, raised $800 million in July 2022 at a valuation of $6.7 billion — an 85 percent reduction from its $45.6 billion peak twelve months earlier. Stripe, which had raised at a $95 billion valuation in 2021, was marked down by multiple crossover fund investors to estimated values between $40 and $55 billion. Instacart's IPO at a $9.9 billion valuation confirmed the markdown from its $39 billion peak.

The employee perspective on these markdowns was particularly painful. Thousands of startup employees had received equity compensation in the form of options struck at 2021 valuations. If a company's stock price needed to recover to $100 per share for options struck at $95 to be in the money, and the company was raising new capital at implied per-share prices of $40, those options were deeply underwater with no near-term path to profitability.

The lesson for the next cycle — when enthusiasm inevitably returns — is one that venture capital has failed to internalize repeatedly: valuation is not determined by narrative alone. Companies must eventually generate cash flow to justify the prices investors pay. The interval between funding at a story and being held accountable to a financial reality is merely a function of capital market conditions. When those conditions tighten, the gap between story and reality closes, painfully and often rapidly.
