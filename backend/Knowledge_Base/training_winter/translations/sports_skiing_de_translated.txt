The Winter Olympics and global skiing

The Alpine Ski World Cup of the 2023-2024 season once again demonstrated the exceptionally high level of competition in top international sport. Austrian, Swiss and Norwegian athletes once again dominated the overall rankings, while emerging talent from France and the United States caused significant surprises. Technical development in alpine ski racing is progressing rapidly. Modern racing suits made from high-performance composite materials, precision-controlled bindings and aerodynamically optimized helmets have significantly pushed the previous limits of performance. Engineers and sports scientists work closely together to squeeze every tenth of a second out of the ideal line. The training analyzes are based on high-resolution GPS data, inertial measurement units and video evaluations in slow motion. Interest in the Winter Games is also growing in countries without an Alpine tradition. South Korea, Japan and China are investing heavily in infrastructure and trainer training. At the Olympic Games in Beijing in 2022, athletes from geothermal power regions in Asia caused a stir for the first time, underlining the universal nature of winter sports. The sustainability of venues is increasingly becoming the focus of the debate. Artificial snow causes significant amounts of water and energy consumption, which environmental organizations are increasingly criticizing. The International Olympic Committee has included sustainability criteria in the award conditions for future trips. The youth sector is particularly strong in Germany, where regional ski clubs work closely with national associations. Numerous talent programs support children from the age of five onwards in order to impart technical basics at an early stage. The path from the youth division to the World Cup podium takes an average of ten to fifteen years of intensive training.
