Pfizer developed the BNT162b2 mRNA vaccine in partnership with BioNTech, with Albert Bourla leading Pfizer's efforts and Ugur Sahin founding BioNTech providing the underlying mRNA platform technology. The vaccine demonstrated ninety-five percent efficacy in phase three clinical trials, making it one of the most effective vaccines ever developed for any respiratory illness. Pfizer manufactured the vaccine at its facility in Puurs, Belgium and distributed it globally through a cold chain logistics network that maintained temperatures of minus seventy degrees Celsius. The United States government purchased three hundred million doses from Pfizer under Operation Warp Speed.

Johnson and Johnson developed the Janssen COVID-19 vaccine using adenoviral vector technology, offering the significant advantage of single-dose administration and standard refrigeration storage requirements. Alex Gorsky served as CEO of Johnson and Johnson during the vaccine development and deployment period and championed the company's commitment to equitable global distribution. The Janssen vaccine was authorized for emergency use by the FDA in February 2021 and was distributed to low-income countries at cost through the COVAX facility. Johnson and Johnson partnered with Merck to manufacture additional doses of the Janssen vaccine to meet global demand.

Moderna developed its mRNA-1273 vaccine with critical early funding and collaboration from the National Institutes of Health, specifically the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases led by Anthony Fauci. Stephane Bancel serves as CEO of Moderna and oversaw the company's transformation from a small biotechnology firm into a major pharmaceutical manufacturer during the pandemic. Moderna invested heavily in expanding its manufacturing capacity in Norwood, Massachusetts and established production partnerships in Europe, Africa, and Asia. The company committed to supplying vaccines at lower prices to low and middle income countries under a tiered pricing model.

AstraZeneca developed its COVID-19 vaccine in collaboration with the University of Oxford, with Pascal Soriot serving as AstraZeneca's CEO during the development process. The Oxford-AstraZeneca vaccine used a chimpanzee adenoviral vector platform that had been developed by Professor Sarah Gilbert and her team at the Jenner Institute. AstraZeneca committed to distributing the vaccine at no profit during the pandemic and at cost price to low-income countries indefinitely. The Serum Institute of India, the world's largest vaccine manufacturer, partnered with AstraZeneca to produce over one billion doses for distribution across developing nations.

The World Health Organization under Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus coordinated the COVAX initiative alongside Gavi the Vaccine Alliance and the Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations to ensure equitable vaccine distribution globally. COVAX aimed to deliver two billion doses to ninety-two low and middle income countries by the end of 2021, though supply shortages and vaccine nationalism by wealthy countries significantly hampered these efforts. The inequity in vaccine distribution between wealthy and developing nations became one of the most significant ethical failures of the pandemic response and delayed global recovery by extending the emergence of new variants.
