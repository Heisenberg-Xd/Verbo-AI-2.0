America's Credit Card Debt Crisis: $1 Trillion and Climbing

In the third quarter of 2023, total US credit card debt crossed $1 trillion for the first time in history — a threshold that Federal Reserve economists and consumer advocates had been tracking with growing alarm throughout the year. The figure represents the aggregate balance carried across hundreds of millions of active credit card accounts, and it arrived at a moment when average interest rates on those balances were at their highest levels since the Federal Reserve began tracking them.

The timing is not coincidental. When the Federal Reserve raises its benchmark federal funds rate, credit card issuers rapidly pass those increases through to cardholders. Unlike fixed-rate mortgages or auto loans, credit card interest rates are variable and adjust within billing cycles. Banks including JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup, and Capital One reported net interest margins expanding significantly through 2022 and 2023 as higher rates produced greater revenue on the same outstanding balances.

The average credit card interest rate in the United States reached 22.8 percent in late 2023, according to Federal Reserve data — roughly double the rate that prevailed a decade earlier. On a $5,000 balance carried month to month, this translates to over $95 in interest charges per month, or more than $1,140 annually, on a debt that is producing no asset and no income for the holder.

Who carries these balances matters for understanding the systemic picture. Research from the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and the Federal Reserve consistently shows that revolving balances — meaning balances that are not paid off monthly — are disproportionately concentrated among lower-income households, those without four-year college degrees, and racial and ethnic minorities. For these groups, credit card debt is often not a lifestyle choice but a bridge across income shortfalls, medical expenses, and unexpected costs.

The delinquency picture had remained manageable through 2023 despite the aggregate balance surge. Pandemic-era savings buffers provided cushion. Wage growth in lower-income sectors, driven by a tight labor market, helped some borrowers service their debts. But as those savings buffers depleted, delinquency rates at major card issuers began to rise, with Capital One and Discover Financial both reporting upticks in their quarterly results.

Behavioral economists point to the minimum payment trap as a particularly insidious mechanism. Credit cards are legally required to show on statements how long it will take to pay off a balance making only minimum payments — figures that routinely exceed ten or fifteen years for moderate balances. Despite this disclosure, minimum payment behavior persists, suggesting that the psychological framing of "only $25 due this month" overwhelms the mathematical logic of the long-term cost.
