SoftBank's Vision Fund and the Art of Betting at a Grand Scale

In 2016, Masayoshi Son flew to Trump Tower with a single slide and walked out with a pledge to invest $50 billion in America. It was a typically Son-esque performance — audacious, symbolic, designed to operate at a level of grandiosity that made conventional deal-making look small. What followed was the Vision Fund: a $97 billion vehicle, largely funded by Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund and the Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund, that would spend four years rewriting the rules of late-stage venture capital.

The Vision Fund's operating philosophy was explicitly about pace and scale over price discipline. Son believed that in winner-take-all markets, the company with the most capital could establish dominance by subsidizing growth faster than competitors or incumbents could respond. He called this strategy "blitzscaling" before it had that name. His ambition was a thirty-year horizon in which AI and robotics would generate returns of a magnitude that conventional portfolio management could not contemplate.

The vision produced some historic hits. SoftBank invested in Alibaba in 2000 at a valuation of around $20 million; that position became worth over $100 billion. The Vision Fund's investment in ByteDance, TikTok's parent company, returned multiples on paper. ARM Holdings, the British chip IP company that SoftBank acquired for $32 billion in 2016, went public in 2023 at a valuation exceeding $54 billion after a proposed $40 billion sale to Nvidia collapsed under antitrust scrutiny.

But the Vision Fund became equally famous for its investments in companies that destroyed extraordinary amounts of capital. WeWork, which SoftBank funded at a cumulative valuation of $47 billion before an attempted IPO revealed deepening losses and corporate governance failures, became the symbol of late-cycle excess. Adam Neumann's charismatic pitch had persuaded Son to write increasingly large checks into a real estate company dressed in the language of a technology platform. The resulting collapse cost SoftBank billions in direct losses and contributed to Son's admitting that he had been "foolish" in his investment assessment.

Katerra, a construction technology company backed by $1.5 billion from Vision Fund, filed for bankruptcy in 2021. OYO, the Indian hospitality company, went public at a significantly reduced valuation after Vision Fund had invested at a $10 billion peak. The portfolio's inconsistency — world-beating winners alongside companies that should never have received nine-figure checks — illustrated the fundamental tension in Son's approach.

After posting $27 billion in losses in fiscal year 2022, SoftBank retreated dramatically. Son declared the company in "defense mode" and Vision Fund 2 significantly reduced its deployment pace. By 2024, Son was resurfacing with renewed focus on artificial intelligence, particularly infrastructure investments in data centers and AI chip plants. Whether the Vision Fund approach — betting on paradigm-shifting technology at unprecedented scale — produces returns commensurate with its risk over a full cycle remains the essential unanswered question of modern venture capital.
