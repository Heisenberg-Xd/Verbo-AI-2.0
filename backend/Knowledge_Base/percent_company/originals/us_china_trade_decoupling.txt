US-China Economic Decoupling: Tariffs, Technology Bans, and the Rewiring of Global Supply Chains

The trade war between the United States and China that escalated under the Trump administration in 2018, and that the Biden administration largely preserved and extended in different forms, has accelerated a structural decoupling of the two largest economies that was already underway before the tariffs began. What started as a dispute over bilateral trade deficits has evolved into a comprehensive competition over technology leadership, military capability, and the rules governing the global economy.

The tariff architecture constructed between 2018 and 2020 imposed 25 percent duties on roughly $370 billion of Chinese goods imported into the United States, affecting everything from steel and aluminum to consumer electronics and industrial machinery. China retaliated with tariffs on approximately $110 billion of US exports, targeting agricultural products — soybeans, pork, and cotton — in ways calculated to inflict maximum political pain on American farm states.

The technology dimension escalated in 2022 and 2023 into something more consequential than tariffs. The US Commerce Department's export controls on advanced semiconductors and chip manufacturing equipment to China represented a qualitatively different intervention — an effort not merely to price-disadvantage Chinese producers but to prevent them from accessing the tools needed to manufacture certain categories of advanced chips at all. Companies including Nvidia, AMD, and Intel were required to restrict sales of their highest-performance AI accelerators to Chinese customers, even as their consumer graphics card businesses continued normal operations.

American chip equipment makers — Applied Materials, Lam Research, and KLA Corporation — applied their own export restrictions after government direction, preventing Chinese chipmakers like SMIC from accessing the litho machines and deposition equipment required to manufacture cutting-edge semiconductor nodes. ASML, the Dutch maker of extreme ultraviolet lithography equipment, was similarly blocked from selling its most advanced machines to Chinese customers under US pressure on the Dutch government, even though the company is not American-owned.

The supply chain response from multinational corporations has been the most economically significant consequence of the decoupling dynamic. Apple accelerated its efforts to diversify iPhone manufacturing to India, with Foxconn and Tata establishing production lines in Tamil Nadu and other Indian states. Vietnam emerged as the primary beneficiary of electronic assembly relocation, with Samsung, LG, and numerous contract manufacturers growing their Vietnamese footprints dramatically. The descriptor "China-plus-one" became ubiquitous in supply chain strategy presentations throughout the business world.

Whether full decoupling is economically feasible remains debated. China manufactures over 80 percent of the world's lithium-ion batteries, controls processing of most rare earth minerals, and remains the only market capable of producing many consumer electronics at existing price points and scale. Supply chain independence from China would require decades and trillions in capital expenditure. But the direction of travel — toward more distributed, resilient, and politically aligned supply chains — appears durable regardless of which political party governs Washington.
