Recession Watch 2024: Reading the Economic Indicators That Actually Matter

The two-year recession that institutional forecasters predicted would follow the Federal Reserve's aggressive rate hikes has not arrived. The US economy expanded at a 3.3 percent annualized rate in the fourth quarter of 2023, defying the predictions of economists who, watching the most inverted yield curve since the early 1980s, declared a recession statistically inevitable. The labor market added an average of over 250,000 jobs per month throughout 2023. Consumer spending, the engine of two-thirds of US GDP, remained resilient.

None of this means recession risk has vanished. Economic forecasters have a poor track record of predicting recessions with precision — they tend to see them clearly only in the rearview mirror. The tools available for real-time recession probability estimation each have known weaknesses, but studying them together provides a more complete picture than any single indicator.

The yield curve inversion — specifically the spread between 10-year and 2-year Treasury yields — has inverted before every US recession since 1955, with no false positives. By this measure, the recession signal fired in March 2022 and remained active through early 2024. The historical lag between inversion and the onset of recession has ranged from six months to twenty-four months, meaning the signal was telling us to expect recession somewhere between late 2022 and early 2025. Whether this time is different, or merely delayed, remains the central macroeconomic question.

The Conference Board's Leading Economic Index, a composite of ten forward-looking indicators, had declined for twenty-four consecutive months through late 2023 — one of the longest sustained declines in the index's history. Past instances of such extended decline have reliably preceded recessions. The ISM Manufacturing Index remained below the critical 50 threshold throughout most of 2023, indicating contraction in industrial activity. Commercial real estate stress, particularly in the office sector, created growing concerns about regional bank exposure that echoed the pattern of prior financial cycle downturns.

Consumer credit delinquencies, particularly in the auto loan and credit card categories, rose from historically low post-pandemic levels toward more normal ranges through 2023. The delinquency trajectory has not yet reached panic thresholds, but the direction raises questions about whether the consumer's resilience is durable or depleting.

The most defensible conclusion from this evidence mosaic is one of genuine uncertainty. The economy has proven more resilient than most predicted, but the lagged effects of monetary tightening may still arrive. Investors preparing for either scenario — extending duration in bonds if recession comes, maintaining equity exposure if the soft landing holds — face a genuinely unusual environment in which both outcomes remain plausible well into 2024.
